Key principle: Each camera angle joins Zoom as a separate participant with its own dedicated computer. This gives the production team individual ISO feeds for clean switching and post-production flexibility.
Zoom uses UDP for media streams ‚Äî some corporate firewalls block UDP or rate-limit it, causing choppy video and audio that's nearly impossible to diagnose without knowing this.

Required open ports: UDP 3478, 3479, 8801‚Äì8802. TCP 443 as fallback (Zoom auto-downgrades to TCP if UDP is blocked, but quality degrades).

Bandwidth requirements per computer: 1080p30 upload requires minimum 3 Mbps sustained. Plan for 10 Mbps per machine with headroom for burst. On a 4-camera setup: minimum 40 Mbps upload at the remote location ‚Äî test this specifically, not just headline ISP speed.

QoS: if the venue has a managed network, request that UDP traffic be prioritized (DSCP marking EF / Expedited Forwarding). A QoS policy that deprioritizes Zoom traffic in favor of other hotel systems will cause dropout even on a nominally fast connection.

VPN: if the presenter's computer has a VPN active, disable it for the duration of the show. Most VPNs route all traffic through a single tunnel, adding latency and often capping bandwidth. Corporate laptops may re-enable VPN automatically ‚Äî confirm it's off immediately before going live.
Cameras

Camera Positions

Recommended angles to give the production team flexible cutting options

CAM 1

Wide / Establishing

Full space + presenter in frame. Shows context ‚Äî the lab, field site. Use a tripod. This is your safety shot.

CAM 2

Medium / Presenter

Chest-to-head framing. Primary talking-head shot. Slightly off-center ‚Äî leaves room for lower thirds.

CAM 3

Close-up of the subject: equipment, specimens, screens. Allows the stage presenter to reference specific items.

CAM 4

Optional. Secondary person or roving shot. Pre-agree with production team. No surprise framing changes.

Network: Wired ethernet required. WiFi is not acceptable for production. Minimum 10 Mbps upload per computer. Test 24 hours before the event.
For broadcast or prosumer cameras (Sony, Canon, Blackmagic, Panasonic PTZ), use a capture card to bring the HDMI or SDI signal into the computer as a virtual USB camera device.

Recommended capture cards: Elgato Cam Link 4K ¬∑ Blackmagic UltraStudio ¬∑ Magewell USB Capture HDMI

Set the camera output to 1080p30 and enable clean HDMI output ‚Äî disable all on-screen overlays (focus peaking, zebras, histogram, battery indicators) before they reach the output signal. Zoom will capture these as part of the image.

If the camera outputs 1080i (interlaced): confirm the capture card deinterlaces to 1080p before passing to Zoom. Interlaced video over Zoom creates comb artifacts on any fast movement in frame.

Power: capture cards draw USB bus power ‚Äî use a powered USB hub if running multiple USB devices on the same machine. Bus power starvation causes capture cards to drop frames or disconnect mid-show.

RECOMMENDED MIC TYPES

Clip to lapel, 6‚Äì8" below chin. Presenter can move freely. Requires bodypack transmitter + USB receiver into computer.

Keeps consistent distance from mouth. Great for stationary presenters or hands-on demos.

ZOOM AUDIO SETTINGS ‚Äî ISO PRODUCTION
  • ‚úì
    Original Sound ‚Üí ON
    Most important setting. Bypasses all of Zoom's audio processing ‚Äî AGC, noise suppression, echo cancellation. Your hardware interface provides the signal; Zoom just passes it through. Find it in Zoom ‚Üí Audio ‚Üí Music and Professional Audio ‚Üí "Show in-meeting option to enable Original Sound" then enable it in the meeting itself from the top bar.
  • ‚úì
    High Fidelity Music Mode ‚Üí ON
    Increases Zoom's audio codec from ~32kbps to ~96kbps and disables the audio processing pipeline. Required for broadcast-quality ISO. Enable under Zoom ‚Üí Audio ‚Üí Music and Professional Audio.
  • ‚úì
    Echo Cancellation ‚Üí OFF (not Auto)
    Auto means Zoom decides when to apply it ‚Äî unacceptable for production. Set to OFF explicitly. With Original Sound enabled this is overridden anyway, but set it manually as a safety net.
  • ‚úì
    Suppress Background Noise ‚Üí None / Disabled
    Zoom's noise suppression adds artifacts and latency. Your hardware chain handles noise ‚Äî not Zoom.
  • ‚úì
    Automatic Gain Control (AGC) ‚Üí OFF
    AGC rides input gain automatically and will fight your hardware preamp setting. Causes pumping and level instability. Must be disabled when using an audio interface with fixed gain staging.
  • ‚úì
    Stereo Audio ‚Üí OFF for speech, ON for stereo sources
    A single lav mic is mono ‚Äî stereo OFF. If using a stereo ambient mic or stereo playback source, enable it. Don't leave it ON by default ‚Äî it doubles the audio data unnecessarily for mono sources.
  • ‚úì
    Input Level ‚Üí peaks at ‚àí12 dBFS
    Set at the hardware interface preamp ‚Äî not Zoom's software gain slider. Verify with FOH the day before, not on show day.
üéõÔ∏è
Original Sound in-meeting: After joining the Zoom call, confirm the "Original Sound: On" button appears in the top-left of the Zoom window and is active. It resets between meetings ‚Äî check it every show, not just in settings.
üî¥
Critical ‚Äî Audio Assignment: Only ONE computer at the remote location should transmit audio ‚Äî the one connected to the presenter's mic. All other camera computers must be muted in Zoom. This prevents echo, phase issues, and duplicate audio at the mix position.
For professional wireless lav systems with XLR receiver output (Shure, Sennheiser, Audio-Technica), use a small USB audio interface to get the signal into the computer cleanly.

Recommended interfaces: Focusrite Scarlett Solo ¬∑ MOTU M2 ¬∑ Zoom H6 (interface mode)

Signal chain: Wireless receiver XLR out ‚Üí Audio interface XLR in ‚Üí USB to computer ‚Üí select as mic input in Zoom

Gain staging: set the interface preamp gain so the Zoom input meter peaks around ‚Äì12 dBFS during normal speech. Don't rely on Zoom's software input gain ‚Äî set it correctly at the hardware preamp.

Phantom power: condenser mics require 48V phantom power from the interface. Dynamic mics and wireless receiver outputs do NOT need phantom power ‚Äî enabling it on an XLR line-level source won't damage most gear, but turn it off to be safe.

Driver note: on Windows, install the ASIO driver for your interface for lowest latency. On Mac, Core Audio is used automatically ‚Äî no driver needed for most interfaces.
Monitoring

IFB & Stage Audio Return

The remote presenter must hear the stage clearly ‚Äî poor monitoring breaks conversation flow

01
Use a single earbud ‚Äî not headphones
One ear open keeps the presenter aware of their local environment. A wired or wireless IEM earbud is fine. Avoid over-ear headphones that look unnatural on camera.
02
Audio feed source
The Zoom client on the audio computer outputs the stage mix through its audio output. Route this to the earbud via a headphone amp or directly from the 3.5mm jack.
03
Monitor volume discipline
Keep level conservative ‚Äî just loud enough to hear clearly. Excessive volume bleeds into the mic and causes feedback or echo artifacts at the venue mix position.
04
Pre-show IFB check
Confirm the remote presenter can clearly hear: (1) the stage presenter speaking, and (2) audience reactions that cue them to when they're live. Do this before doors open.
LATENCY AWARENESS

Expect 150‚Äì400ms round-trip latency via Zoom. Both presenters should be briefed before the conversation. Natural 1‚Äì2 second pauses prevent cross-talk confusion. Think of it like a satellite phone call.

On-Camera Presence

Eyeline & Framing

Small adjustments create dramatically more engaging remote presence on a large LED wall

‚úì DO THIS
Look at the camera lens
Tape a small arrow above the lens to remind you. Eye contact with the lens reads as eye contact to the audience.
Camera at eye level
Stack books or use a tripod. Camera below eye level looks up your nose. Above creates a submissive look.
18‚Äì24" from camera
Natural medium framing. Leave headroom above and keep eyes in the upper third of frame.
Stay on camera when listening
React naturally. Don't check notes or look away ‚Äî you're still on screen.
Pause before speaking
Wait 1‚Äì2 seconds after the stage presenter finishes. Latency means responses can overlap if you start immediately.
‚úï AVOID
Reading notes off-screen
Eyes darting sideways looks disengaged on a 20-foot LED wall. Internalize key points.
Cluttered backgrounds
Clear the space behind you: no equipment piles, open doors, or moving people.
Excessive movement
Camera operators can't always follow sudden movement. Agree on a position and hold it.
Speaking before cued
Wait for a clear cue that you're live. Unintentional lead-ins will be heard at the venue before the presenter is ready.
Slouching or looking down
Poor posture looks disengaged on a large screen. Sit upright ‚Äî the LED wall magnifies everything.
